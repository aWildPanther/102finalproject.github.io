Home Automation

Home automation faces challenges with devices like cameras, speakers, and smart locks that collect personal data, such as schedules, video footage, and voice recordings. If not properly secured, these devices are at risk of hacking or unauthorized surveillance. Hackers have been known to access smart cameras and doorbells to monitor residents and their comings and goings. This can expose homeowners to privacy violations and theft. Smart home technology can lead to security vulnerabilities and distrust, which deters potential users. On the other hand, It can also lead to an improved quality of life because it offers convenience and safety, such as remotely controlling things like lights, locks, and appliances. Cameras and smart locks that are controlled remotely can provide real-time monitoring and improve home safety. Home automation comes at a cost. Many of these systems are expensive, which makes them inaccessible to lower-income families. Smart devices are not standardized, so they may not all work together. This can cause frustration for the user and limits scalability. The more devices connected, the higher the chance for these devices to be hacked. Taking security measures is essential, but not all users know how to take security measures. Home automation brings convenience, energy efficiency, and security but comes with challenges like data security, affordability, and security.

Smart Agriculture

The complexities and social challenges of smart agriculture are significant. Small farmers in low to middle-income countries lack the financial resources to adopt IoT farming systems. This challenge means that farmers have unequal access to the productivity gap between larger farms and smaller ones. IoT systems can collect large amounts of data on farming soil, weather, and crop health. This data is often stored on third-party cloud servers. These types of systems may challenge farmers' data privacy and ownership, which could result in exploitation or unfair practices by other agriculture companies. IoT systems can also bring environmental concerns. Using sensors and drones leaves a carbon footprint and, if not appropriately managed, contributes to e-waste. The global population is growing, and agriculture needs to keep up with food demands. Smart agriculture can help by improving crop returns and resource efficiency. Sensors for monitoring soil, weather, crop health, and livestock tracking can help reduce farming costs and make farming more profitable, especially for lower to middle-income farmers. Smart agriculture seems right, but the initial costs can be steep. There is a significant investment in equipment, infrastructure, and training, which most small farmers may be unable to afford. The infrastructure also faces a significant challenge. Most farms are in rural areas where reliable internet and power are not always found. Another challenge is the technical skills needed to operate and maintain IoT systems. Users must know how to connect, troubleshoot, and manage applications.
